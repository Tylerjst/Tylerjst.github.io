Triton Flood Management

Modeling Flood Vulnerability and Bolstering Climate Resilience

My team was tasked to propose a design solution utilizing mobile LIDAR technology created by KAARTA, a local organization in Pittsburgh, PA. Triton is a hypothesized platform to model flood risks and coordinate response efforts between municipal governments and disaster relief organizations while establishing a link with the general public.

I. Overview

The Challenge

Flooding is a real problem. Floods cost the U.S. about $4.3 billion per event in damages and cause more deaths annually than any other natural disaster. These disasters will only worsen with time as Climate Change will drive sea level rise, increasing the threat of powerful storm surges and heavy rainfall. Further, experts' current tools are insufficient to meet the demands that the next generation of floods will bring.


Because of the vast amount of time and labor required to create flood models, "Even year-old maps are outdated due to the speed of climate changes."
Civil Engineer from Sherwood Engineers



Our Solution

TRITON -- an intelligent mapping software that uses real-time data to analyze existing infrastructure and predict flooding likelihood. Using data collected by KAARTA's LIDAR devices mounted onto city vehicles, Triton creates a common platform of consistently updated information for key stakeholders (such as city governments or disaster relief organizations) to collaborate and coordinate efforts.




So What?

This was a conceptual design, and we weren't able to get back in touch with our expert civil engineers. We also never got a chance to reconnect with KAARTA.

II. Solution Highlights

Concept Video




Computer Vision Fleet

To collect data on the city's infrastructure, we envisioned equipping the fleet of city vehicles with KAARTA's LIDAR devices. These scanners would create 3D point clouds of the environment and, when combined with Computer Vision technology, could be trained to spot surface features that indiate deteriorating infrastrcuture that poses flood risks.




Desktop Portal

The desktop portal allows users to access cloud-enabled geospatial data in a 2D, editable view. The software also enables users to collaborate across organizations, ensuring information and data can be easily accessed by the appropriate stakeholder.




Storm Mode

Storm Mode is a predictive modeling feature inside the Desktop Portal that predicts the likelihood of damage and other problems based on given inputs. For instance, a user could select a predicted precipitation level to estimate and envision the impact of a given storm on a particular area. From this mode, users can also push alerts to public TRITON users in areas that are expected to see dangerous effects of flooding.




Public Users & Localized Alerts

TRITON isn't solely designed for government agencies. Putting power in the hands of everyday citizens equips them with additional knowledge about threats to their communities. Users can "opt in" to the TRITON system, gaining access to a "public view" as well as localized alerts when flooding is predicted to cause adverse near their current (or previously established) home location.

III. The Process

Discovering the Problem Space

Our design process was a little unconventional due to the fact that we were creating a conceptual design. My team entered the project knowing we were partnering with KAARTA, a Pittsburgh company specializing in LIDAR devices and 3D models. We began our process by conducting a literature review of Computer Vision to learn of its opportunities and synthesize a problem space.

Generative Research Methods

  • Literature Reviews
  • Expert Interviews
  • Analogous Domains


We felt as though there was great opportunity in the intersection of city ordinance and disaster relief. Our literature review had exposed us to several papers about how Computer Vision was used to analyze city infrastructure in construction zones. Other papers detailed how Computer Vision had potential to identify points of interest in disaster zones to aid relief organizations.

Given our shared interest in these domains, it was a lock for further research.




Expert Interviews

We set out to find experts in these fields to interview and identify pain points to design arounds. Thanks to some connections, we interviewed two Civil Engineers.




Civil Engineer from Sherwood Engineers

It is "massively time consuming" to create flood models of a site and properly map data.

Civil Engineer from U.S. Army Corps of Engineers

Water and flood control structures are inspected only "once every three years."



From these interviews it was clear that a big pain point in disaster management was keeping updating maps and models on affected areas. Currently, many civil engineers and urban designers use tools like ArcGIS. The Civil Engineer from Sherwood Engineers describes these tools as "inaccesible" to all but expert users. Given KAARTA's proficencies, developing accesible tools to model floods seemed like an excellent design opportunity.

Analogous Domains

Alongside our interviews, we looked at some unconventional inspiration in "analogous domains."

The idea of scanning environments for surface features that would generate deeper insights felt familiar. Sure enough, this was a similar feature to Batman's "detective mode" in the Arkham series of video games. Not unlike the Computer Vision technology we were research, Batman could active a scanner that would identify an item or surface of interest, scan it for deeper information, and then perform an array of magically inspired analysis like recreate bullet casings, track footprints, or in the coolest way, reconstruct actual events that had transpired.





Most of this is of course science fiction, but the designs inspired us to look at more 4D models of environments grounded in scanned data. MIT's Media Lab features a project called CityScope that allows users to create interactive models of cities with legos. As users build and break apart certain bricks, a camera registers the particular type of brick and a projector accordingly depicts the part of the urban design that the user is trying to emulate.

These interactive models would ultimately inspire our first prototypes.



Prototyping

When it came time to prototype, our professor gave us metal wire to construct our intial interation. Given that it was likely our final iteration would be a digital product, this was a unique challenge.

Since we couldn't make anything related to screens with the wire, we ended up coming up with a Augmented Reality system where users could interact with a fully scalable 4D depiction of a city block. Similar to Batman's "Dectective Mode" and MIT Media Lab's CityScope, we envisioned this interactive model featuring colored pins that noted infrastructure damage and flooding hazards.





Our body-storming/experience prototyping exercise revealed potential for interaction with otherwise cumbersome data. It also provided us with key insights for futher protoypes that would be required to enable this sort of interactive system. We drafted a project roadmap to capture our ideas.

As cool as our 4D interactive map was, we couldn't find a way to create a prototype of high enough fidelty to test with users given the time restraints of our project. In addition, we noticed this sort of collaborative system would require a cloud service to store data in a centralized place so that different users could access the same data layers using different platforms. Luckily, KAARTA already offers this kind of service.

Therefore, what made most sense for us to continue prototyping was a 2D digital layer similar to ArcGIS or other mapping software. We could easily prototype screens for this idea and test them through Hueristic Evaluations and Think Alouds.




Final Iteration and Presentation

Our final iteration was presented to our class along with our concept video. Unfortunately, we were not able to present our project to KAARTA due to communication failures between our professor and the organization.

IV. Concluding Thoughts

It was a bummer we couldn't present our final iteration to KAARTA. Our team was interested in getting feedback, particularly regarding our idea to partner with city governments. In addition, we were unable to get back in touch with our Civil Engineers that we spoke to during our Discovery phase. We would have been interested to hear their thoughts on our prototypes. Understanding if our desktop portal had any usefulness in the current ecosystem of the municipal government's technical tools would have been our research goal moving forward.

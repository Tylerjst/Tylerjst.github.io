CMU Shuttle Service Stop Signs

Improving the visibility of the CMU Shuttle Service

To improve the visibility and ridership of the Carnegie Mellon University Shuttle Service, our team presented a design for off-campus shuttle stop signs to the Graduate Student Assembly transportation working group.

I. Overview

The Challenge

CMU’s shuttle and escort service isn’t doing so great. Ridership on all scales has dropped by 11% in the past 4 years. GSA’s transportation working group solicited help to increase ridership as well as address other goals, such as

  • Improving access to transportation options for students to and from campus,
  • Increasing the visibility of the shuttle and escort service.



Our Solution

Through my team's research, we found that improving the visibility of the shuttle service through physical/environmental means would also boost ridership of the service. Therefore, we designed a bus stop sign intended for off campus locations that include the bus routes that pass by that stop and the time until the bus arrives.



So What?

Upon testing our prototype, we learned that users connected the new stop signs to CMU's shuttles, thereby improving the visbility of the system. Further, users mentioned they were more likely to use the shuttles with the additional information the signs provided, accomplishing GSA's goals.

II. Solution Highlights

Design Highlights

  • Prominent CMU branding to establish connection the university
  • Illuminated circles around active bus routes
  • Timers counting down until the next bus arrives at stop
  • The shuttle service's online URL if users need additional information


III. The Process

Generative Research

To investigate the shuttle system, we began with generative research methods to find pain points and areas for improvement among students. During our initial generative research, we discovered that many students didn’t even know that the shuttle exists, or they had misunderstandings about the service. We wanted our later rounds of generative research to investigate the roots of these problems more specifically so we could design a solution, then use evaluative research methods to validate our solution.

Generative Research Methods

  • Contextual Inquiry
  • Affinity Diagramming
  • Surveys


By the end of our generative research and subsequent analysis, we discovered the following insights

  1. Few students knew about the CMU Shuttle & Escort Service before they needed to use it.
  2. Students primarily learned about the CMU Shuttle & Escort Service through their friends.
  3. Students also gained information about transportation services from seeing physical buses, information at bus stops, or directly observing how others use the services.
  4. When the students wanted to use the service, they had trouble finding details of the service, including locations for the bus stops.

Storyboarding & Evaluative Research

We used the results from our generative research to produce a series of need-based storyboards and effectively bridge the gap between our generative and evaluative methods.

Evaluative Research Methods

  • Speed Dating
  • Think Alouds
  • Five Second Tests
  • Experience Prototyping




We conducted speed-dating sessions with 5 participants, walking them through each storyboard and asked follow-up questions to gauge reactions. This allowed us to validate whether we were addressing the correct needs of our population while also directing us to the most effective design solution.



Prototyping

From our insights, we found that a physical artifact that users could interact in their environment would be most effective in increasing the visibility of the CMU shuttle system. We set out to brainstorm several designs for the bus stop signs before iterating on our ultimate choice.







Final Iteration and Presentation

Our final iteration was presented along with the rest of our cohort in our User Research Methods course. introduced our solution to our professors as well as the GSA transportation working group.

IV. Concluding Thoughts

There were opportunities to iterate upon our final design. For instance, GSA representatives mentioned laws regulating the placement of bus stop signs around Pittsburgh that would limit the locations where off-campus bus stop signs could be installed. Further, we found through experience prototypting that users had a hard time distinguishing the illuminated bus routes and even making sense of the bus route names. A new way of displaying active bus routes and labeling the bus routes are areas of opportunity for any future work.

Block the Vote

Spreading awareness of American voter supression

I was assigned to create an informational website for my Programmable User Interfaces class in Fall 2018. Since the 2018 Midterm Elections were right around the corner, and because my registered state of Georgia was in the spotlight for enacting racist voter policies, I decided to make a site about the dangers of voter suppression.

I. Overview

The Challenge

While not as flagrant as during the Jim Crow era, voter suppresion is alive and well in modern America. A number of policies -- from Voter ID laws to reducing the number of voter precincts -- limits access to voting and shrinks participation. Meanwhile, unsubstantianted myths about voter fraud circulate social media and right-wing news outlets. How could I address both misinformation and provide evidence for the various sources of voter suppression that wont cause reader's eyeballs to roll into the back of their heads?



The Solution

A minimalist website with a strong emphasis on visual/geospatial representations and indicators of voter suppression.

So What?

It's safe to say my site didn't influence any elections, but the research required to visualize this data exposed a large gap in our collective understand of voting. For example, I would have loved to compare the number of voting locations in 2018 vs 2016 by county, but access to that data is obtuse or non-existant. Other ideas that I was unable to explore due to limited time or access were:

  1. Mapping polling locations for county polling locations and calculating the average distance for registered voters,
  2. Visualizing the ratio of polling locations to voter turnout,
  3. and vizualizing average wait times for each polling location.

II. Solution Highlights

Dispelling Voter Fraud Myths

The splash page features a carousel of statistics to dispel myths about the prevelance of voter fraud -- a common argument in favor of tactics that suppress turnout.




Voter ID Laws

ID requirements change state by state. However, strict ID laws are one of the most common and easiest ways to disenfranchise voters. Knowing what type of requirement your state features can empower a voter to ensure their vote gets counted.




Voter Roll Purges

Removing inactive voters from registered databases is another common way states can inhibit voting. This tactic recently gained coverage in mainstream media, but knowing which states engaged in the practice could awaken people to the pervasiveness. Addition data could have been used here to visually compare the number of incorrectly purged voters from the total number of purged voters.




Side Menu

The minimalist aesthetic of the site was meant to emphasize the factual basis of voter suppression. No frills should be necessary to convince someone why this problem is seriously. Considering that, I wanted all attention to be on the narrative, but users could jump to any page at any point of the site.

III. Concluding thoughts

This assignment became more of a research project than an exercise in web design. There is tons of information related to voter supression out there on there internet -- as well as plenty of blank spots that would illuminate major problem areas. For example, Georgia does not offer explicit data about the number of voting precints per year or the average wait times at each precinct.

This site offers a lot of opporunity to continue exploring visualizing the mechanisms of voter suppression. If there's anything my tour around the internet showed me, it's that there is a dearth of well designed visualizations on this topic. Increasing the number may lead to more exposure and better understanding of the issue.

Future opportunities in this space include:

  1. Mapping polling locations for county polling locations and calculating the average distance for registered voters.
  2. Visualizing the ratio of polling locations to voter turnout.
  3. Visualizing the reduction of polling locations.
  4. Calculating and vizualizing average wait times for each polling location.

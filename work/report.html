I. The Mission

When I was a summer intern for the Office of Sustainability Iniatives in 2015, the office was celebrating their 10 year anniversary. As they began to turn towards the future with a new sustainability vision, they wanted to highlight their acheivements and progress in the past 10 years.

I was tasked with developing a report for these highlights. The director of OSI asked that the report look "flashy" and gave me other examples from other universities to benchmark. Otherwise, the rest was up to me.

II. The Report

While benchmarking other universities, I noticed that schools like Harvard University used a mobile-first HTML style of design. They built webpages that were mobile responsive, interactive, and created a full user experience. Based on this, I knew that an HTML report would satisfy the director's requests, but would also allow the office to incoporate a more modern aesthetic. By using minimalist design to construct the report and visualize data, I figured audiences would begin to associate OSI, and sustainability, with something trendy and modern.

Our Roots Run Deep

I ended up building a custom document using the bootstrap framework. Since there was a large amount of data and relatively little structure to it, I divided the information into 5 sections:

  • Campus Operations
  • Energy, Water, and Carbon
  • Sustainable Food
  • Recycling and Reuse
  • Education and Outreach
Within the sections, the information was split between qualitative and quantitative information. I again had to figure out how to display that information effectively.

III. The Data

Ultimately, I followed that dichotomy of quantitative vs qualitative to break each section into smaller subsections. At the top, graphs and charts (built with Google Charts API) to vizualize the data. Next, big, qualitative iniatives that contextualize the data. Finally, smaller, rapid-fire accomplishments that OSI wanted to highlight.

In the end, the structure seemed to work very well. Unfortunately, the document was never put to use.

Sustainability Education 10-Year Reports & Infographics

A 10-Year report highlighting an office's history sparked a series of experiments in sustainability branding, marketing, and education...



I. Minority Report

When I was a summer intern for the Office of Sustainability Iniatives in 2015, the office was celebrating their 10 year anniversary. As they began to turn towards the future with a new sustainability vision and goals, they wanted to highlight their acheivements and progress in the past 10 years.

I was tasked with developing a report for these highlights. The director of OSI asked that the report looked "flashy" and gave me other examples from other universities to benchmark; otherwise, the rest was up to me.

After benchmarking other universities, I decided that an html report would satisfy the director's requests, but would also allow the office to incoporate more modern design aesthetics. By using minimalist, modern design to construct the report and visualize data, I figured audiences would begin to associate OSI, and sustainability, with something trendy and modern.

II. The Move-In List

While developing this philosophy for the sustainability report, I noticed opportunties to spread it into my other work. That same summer, I was also working as an intern for the Office of Residence Life. One of my projects was to create an infographic instructing students what to bring with them as they moved onto campus.

While creating the list, I decided to replace generic items and offer sustainable options instead, indicating the different choices with the OSI oak leaf. In addition, I met with the manager of the campus Barnes & Noble to place stickers of the OSI leaf next to sustainable options in the bookstore.

The intent, similar to the sustainability report, was that by creating associating easily indentifiable logo (the oak leaf) with sustainable options or features on the list, people would be able to recognize sustainable options in stores or around campus. Whereever they would see the leaf, they would know that a sustainability feature exists in that space.

This would hopefully boost the visibility of sustainability on campus and give people a greater awareness and an introduction into broader topics.

III. The List Strikes Back

Ultimately, the Move-In list served to "tell" people what sustainable options to buy, but it didn't "show" them why they should that, or why they should even care. That prompted the creation of the "back-side" of the Move-In list.

This flip-side of the document was designed to break down bigger topics in sustainability that touched on why certain items were marked as sustainable options. These included water bottles (water conservation), recycled paper (waste), and micro-pellet free soap (biodiversity).

Since most people weren't going to take the time to read through these chunks of information, I decided to package each topic in an infographic. These were sent out via social media, and embodied the philosophy shared with the preceeding projects.

Previously, sustainability at Emory was stuck with outdated design that look straight out of the 90s. I wanted to change that to match the forward-thinking work with an equally modern aesthetic. That, I hoped, would engage more of campus to get invovled and commmunicate information more effectively.

Sustainability Education at Emory University

Creating shopping lists and infographics for Emory students

A sustainable shopping list for incoming first-year students at Emory University sparked a series of experiments in sustainability branding, a partnership with the on-campus Barnes & Noble, and infographics.


Duration

3 Months

(May 2015 - Aug 2015)

Team

Tina Chang

Tyler Stern

Clients

Emory Residence Life

Emory Office of Sustainability Initiatives

Barnes & Noble

My Role

Visual Design


I. Overview

The Challenge

It's the summer of 2015, and in a few months, thousands of first year students will arrive at Emory University to begin their undergraduate experience. The Office of Residence Life is in the business of making living on campus as joyous as possible, so they wanted a way to facillitate the transition from living at home to living at Emory as painless as possible.

That same summer, I was also working as an intern for the Office of Sustainability Iniatives. Their goal was to communicate Emory's sustainability values to first year students as early as possible to begin the educaiton process.



The Solution

  1. A "Sustainable Shopping List" that recommends sustainable products for incoming first year students with the OSI logo: an oak leaf
  2. Physical oak leaves placed near sustainable products in the on-campus Barnes & Noble bookstore.
  3. A "back-side" to the shopping list that explains certain sustainability topics.
  4. 6 infographics that visually explore the topics from the "back-side" of the shopping list.


So What?

II. Solution Highlights

The Move-In List

While creating the list, I decided to replace generic items and offer sustainable options instead, indicating the different choices with the OSI oak leaf. In addition, I met with the manager of the campus Barnes & Noble to place stickers of the OSI leaf next to sustainable options in the bookstore.



The "Back-Side"

The flip-side of the document was designed to break down bigger topics in sustainability that touched on why certain items were marked as sustainable options. These included water bottles (water conservation), recycled paper (waste), and micro-pellet free soap (biodiversity). The blurbs were also framed as questions because I wanted to address myths or misunderstandings about sustainability.



The Infographics

That's when I became interested in trying to find a more engaging way of communicating the information on the back of the list. Since most people weren't going to take the time to read through these chunks of information, I decided to package each topic in an infographic. I figured that if someone were to stumble across them om social media (where they were posted), they would see the question that they had been asking themself potentially learn something new about that topic. Perhaps this new found knowledge might spur -- though not necessairly immediately -- a change towards more sustainable behavior.




III. Concluding thoughts

There was no analysis conducted after the release of the list or infographics. The sustainable leafs are still used in the bookstore and the sustainable move-in list is still used on Residence Life's website, but it's not apparent if they have made a difference in the types of items purchased by first-year students. Similarly, there is no indication that the infographics were noticed by social media users. They each received less than 10 likes per post.
